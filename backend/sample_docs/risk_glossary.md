# Risk & Investing Glossary

Standard deviation measures how much an investment's returns swing above and below its average return over time. A higher standard deviation means a bumpier ride, even if the long-run average return is attractive.

Sharpe ratio expresses how much extra return an investment produced for each unit of risk taken, by dividing excess return over a risk-free rate by the investment's standard deviation. Investors generally prefer a higher Sharpe ratio when comparing two options with similar goals.

Diversification is the practice of spreading money across many different assets, sectors, and geographies so that a decline in any single holding has a smaller effect on the overall portfolio. It does not eliminate risk but reduces the impact of any one bad outcome.

Asset allocation refers to the mix of asset classes, such as equities, bonds, cash, and alternatives, held in a portfolio. It is widely considered the single biggest driver of long-term portfolio outcomes, more influential than individual security selection.

Drawdown describes the peak-to-trough decline in the value of a portfolio during a specific period. Investors with a low tolerance for drawdown typically need a larger allocation to lower-volatility assets like short-duration bonds and cash equivalents.

Time horizon is the length of time an investor expects to hold an investment before needing the money. Longer time horizons generally allow for a larger allocation to growth-oriented, higher-volatility assets since there is more time to recover from downturns.

Rebalancing is the process of periodically buying and selling holdings to return a portfolio to its target asset allocation. Without rebalancing, strong-performing assets can grow to dominate a portfolio, unintentionally increasing its risk level.

Dollar-cost averaging means investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals regardless of price, which naturally buys more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high, smoothing out the effect of volatility over time.
